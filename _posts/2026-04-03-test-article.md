A new study out of MIT suggests the prefrontal cortex plays a larger role in belief revision than previously thought — and that emotional state matters more than logic.

Researchers observed that participants were significantly more likely to update their beliefs when in a calm emotional state, regardless of the strength of the argument presented to them.

The implications are significant. If the emotional context in which we receive information determines whether we accept it, then the quality of an argument may matter far less than the moment in which it is delivered.

Which raises an uncomfortable question: how many of our most firmly held beliefs were formed not because the evidence was overwhelming, but because we happened to be in the right state of mind to receive them?
