Since its launch in April 2010, Nature Communications has occupied a unique and often contested space in the landscape of scientific publishing. As the 17th research title established by the Nature Portfolio—a division of Springer Nature—it was the first to operate as an online-only, multidisciplinary venue. Today, it stands as a pillar of the open-access movement, yet its journey from a hybrid experiment to a high-impact powerhouse reveals the complex tensions between scientific accessibility, institutional prestige, and the high cost of academic gatekeeping.

The journal was originally designed to publish high-quality research across the natural sciences, including biological, health, physical, chemical, and Earth sciences. While many Nature-branded journals focus on a narrow niche, Nature Communications was built to be broad, aiming to serve specialists while remaining relevant to the wider scientific community. According to reports from Research Information, the journal initially launched as a hybrid model, allowing authors to choose between subscription-based publishing or paying a fee to make their work open access.

## The Pivot to Open Access

A defining moment for the journal occurred on October 20, 2014, when it stopped accepting subscription-based submissions. This shift made Nature Communications the first major Nature-branded title to commit to a fully open-access model. By January 2016, all of its content became freely accessible to the public.

This transition was framed by leadership as a strategic mission to lead the industry. Sam Burridge, then-managing director for open research at Nature Publishing Group, described the move as putting "a flag in the ground which said we are committed to open access." Carrie Calder, the group’s strategy director for open research, noted that the decision was driven by a perceived demand for a high-quality, Nature-branded open-access outlet. Calder further stated that the model was viewed as "more sustainable for the research community, and particularly for library budgets," given the rapid growth of published literature.

The journal’s impact has grown significantly alongside its scale. In 2013, its Impact Factor was 10.742. By 2024, that figure had risen to 15.7, with a five-year Impact Factor of 17.2. These metrics rank the journal 10th among multidisciplinary science journals globally. This influence is reflected in its reach; according to 2024 data, the journal sees over 177 million annual article downloads.

## Editorial Rigor and the "Dumping Ground" Debate

Despite its high volume—having published over 27,833 articles by April 2020—Nature Communications maintains a reputation for rigorous peer review. The journal employs a large team of in-house professional editors, such as Nathalie Le Bot for health and clinical sciences, to handle the heavy influx of submissions. 

The barrier to entry remains high. Statistics show that the journal has a desk rejection rate between 75% and 92%, with most of these decisions made within eight days of submission. The final acceptance rate is estimated to be around 20%, though some 2025 data suggests it may have dipped as low as 7.7% in certain periods.

However, the journal’s position within the Nature hierarchy has sparked debate among researchers. In academic circles, Nature Communications is sometimes viewed as a "valuable middle-ground" for papers that are scientifically significant but lack the "breakthrough" novelty required for the flagship *Nature* journal. On platforms like Reddit, some academics have described it as a "dumping ground" for high-quality papers that were rejected by more specialized Nature titles like *Nature Medicine* or *Nature Genetics*. As one user, "Redarrow_ok," observed: "I used to discount it, but I see a lot of excellent science published on it. It ends up as the dumping ground for many articles which miss out on Nature."

## The Rising Cost of Publishing

The most persistent criticism of Nature Communications involves its Article Processing Charge (APC). To maintain an open-access model without subscription fees, the journal charges authors to publish. In 2014, the APC was approximately $5,200 USD. By 2026, that cost is expected to rise to approximately $7,350 USD.

While Nature Portfolio offers waivers and participates in many institutional and funder agreements to cover these costs, the high price point remains a point of contention. Critics argue that such fees create a financial barrier for researchers in developing nations or those without massive grant funding. Conversely, the publisher maintains that these fees are necessary to support a professional editorial staff and the infrastructure required to manage tens of thousands of annual submissions while ensuring transparency initiatives, such as the option to publish peer-review history.

The success of the "Communications" brand has led to further expansion. In 2017, the publisher announced "subjournals" including *Communications Biology*, *Chemistry*, and *Physics*. In 2021, *Communications Medicine* was added to the roster, further cementing the brand’s role in the health and clinical sectors.

While Nature Communications champions the democratization of science through open access, we must ask if it is merely democratizing the right to read while further elitizing the right to publish. With article processing charges climbing toward $7,400, the journal risks creating a two-tier system where "global" science is only accessible to those backed by wealthy Western institutions or specific funder agreements. By leveraging the "Nature" brand to command such high fees for papers that were rejected by more specialized titles, the publisher may be less focused on "driving open access forward" and more focused on capturing the overflow of a prestige-obsessed academic market that equates a brand name with scientific merit.
