The 2026 Americas Lodging Investment Summit (ALIS) opened in Los Angeles this week under a cloud of cautious optimism and sharp self-reflection. While the city prepares to host some of the world's most significant sporting events, the mood among hotel owners, developers, and analysts is increasingly focused on a sobering reality: the United States is losing its share of the global tourism market. As industry leaders gathered to discuss the future of hospitality, the consensus was clear—the U.S. has fallen behind, and the upcoming FIFA World Cup might be the last, best chance to catch up.

For decades, the United States was the undisputed primary destination for international travelers. However, post-pandemic recovery patterns have shown a shifting landscape. While domestic travel remained resilient, international arrivals have not returned with the same vigor seen in other regions. During the opening sessions of the summit, speakers pointed to a variety of factors that have allowed European and Asian markets to outpace the American hospitality sector, leaving the U.S. to play a difficult game of catch-up.

## Losing the Global Competitive Edge

The primary concern voiced at the summit is that the U.S. is no longer the "easy" choice for international visitors. Several industry veterans noted that while the U.S. was once the default destination for high-spending travelers from overseas, a combination of factors has made other countries more attractive. Competitors in the Middle East, Europe, and Southeast Asia have invested heavily in modernizing their tourism infrastructure, simplifying visa processes, and launching aggressive marketing campaigns.

In contrast, the U.S. hospitality sector faces significant headwinds. High labor costs and a shortage of workers continue to plague hotel operations, forcing many properties to reduce services while raising room rates. Furthermore, the strength of the U.S. dollar has made a trip to America prohibitively expensive for many middle-class travelers from emerging markets. When a week in Orlando costs twice as much as a week in Tokyo or Lisbon, the choice for many families becomes clear.

## The 2026 World Cup: A Short-Term Surge?

Much of the discussion at ALIS centered on the 2026 FIFA World Cup, which the U.S. will co-host with Canada and Mexico. For the lodging industry, this event represents a massive opportunity to showcase American hospitality on a global stage. The influx of millions of fans is expected to fill hotels across the country, from major hubs like Los Angeles and New York to smaller host cities.

Optimists at the summit argue that the World Cup will serve as a catalyst for investment. They believe the pressure of hosting a global event will force local governments to speed up infrastructure projects, such as airport renovations and public transit expansions. For the hotel industry, the tournament provides a guaranteed period of high occupancy and a platform to re-introduce the "American brand" to a new generation of international travelers.

However, some analysts at the conference warned against over-reliance on a single event. They pointed out that while "mega-events" provide a temporary spike in revenue, they don't always translate into long-term growth. If the visitor experience is marred by long security lines, expensive transportation, or inadequate service, the long-term reputation of the U.S. as a tourist destination could actually suffer.

## Challenges Beyond the Stadium

The summit also highlighted the structural hurdles that are difficult for the private sector to solve alone. Chief among these is the visa wait times for first-time visitors from key markets. Industry groups have been lobbying for years to reduce the backlog, arguing that the months-long wait for an interview is a significant deterrent to travel. 

Safety and public perception also loom large. News coverage of crime in major American cities, whether statistically accurate or not, heavily influences the decisions of international tourists. Hotel developers expressed concern that until the U.S. addresses the perception of safety in its urban cores, high-end international travelers may continue to favor cities in Europe and Asia that are perceived as more secure.

## Diverging Perspectives on Growth

Despite the warnings, the summit was not without its bright spots. Some investors are doubling down on "experiential" travel, moving away from standard business hotels and toward lifestyle brands that offer unique local experiences. There is a growing belief that the modern traveler is looking for more than just a bed; they want a connection to the culture of the city they are visiting.

There is also a divide between the outlook for luxury properties and mid-scale hotels. While the luxury segment continues to see strong demand from wealthy travelers who are less sensitive to price increases, the mid-scale and budget segments are struggling to maintain margins in the face of rising costs. This "barbell" effect in the industry suggests that the future of U.S. tourism may become increasingly polarized, catering to the very wealthy while the average international visitor looks elsewhere.

As the summit continues, the message to the U.S. government and the hospitality industry is clear: the status quo is no longer sufficient. The 2026 World Cup offers a rare window of opportunity, but without addressing the underlying issues of cost, accessibility, and infrastructure, the U.S. risks becoming a secondary player in the global tourism economy. The world is coming to America in 2026; the question is whether they will want to come back in 2027.
