The corridors of power in Washington and the boardrooms of Wall Street have converged on a singular, urgent priority: a new artificial intelligence model capable of identifying—and exploiting—vulnerabilities that have remained hidden for decades. Following a series of emergency meetings convened by the US Treasury and the Federal Reserve, the nation’s largest banks have begun internal testing of Anthropic PBC’s "Mythos," an AI model so potent that its creators have restricted its public release.

The model, also known as Claude Mythos Preview, represents a significant shift in the arms race between cyber defenders and attackers. According to research reported by Bloomberg, the move to integrate this technology is being spearheaded by the highest levels of the US government. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell recently held an urgent meeting with the CEOs of "systemically important financial institutions" to emphasize that the risks posed by AI-driven cyberattacks are no longer theoretical.

## The Birth of Project Glasswing

The rollout of Mythos is being managed through a highly controlled initiative dubbed "Project Glasswing." Under this program, Anthropic has granted restricted access to approximately 40 organizations. The list includes the titans of the American financial system: JPMorgan Chase & Co., Goldman Sachs Group Inc., Citigroup Inc., Bank of America Corp., and Morgan Stanley.

The exclusivity of the project is a response to the model's unprecedented capabilities. Mythos is designed to autonomously detect and potentially exploit sophisticated cybersecurity vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser. To mitigate the risk of the tool falling into the wrong hands, Anthropic is providing $100 million in usage credits and $4 million in direct donations to open-source security organizations to bolster global defenses alongside the bank testing.

National Economic Council director Kevin Hassett underscored the gravity of the situation, stating, "We are taking every step we can to make sure that everybody is safe from these potential risks, including Anthropic agreeing to hold back the public release of the model until our officials have figured everything out." Hassett added that there is "definitely a sense of urgency" within the administration.

## A "Wake-Up Call" for Digital Defense

The technical prowess of Mythos has reportedly stunned security experts. The model has already uncovered thousands of high-severity flaws that evaded previous human and automated detection. Most notably, it identified a 27-year-old vulnerability in OpenBSD and a 16-year-old flaw in FFmpeg. The latter had remarkably remained hidden despite five million previous automated tests.

Alissa Valentina Knight, CEO of cybersecurity AI company Assail, described the current landscape as a point of no return. "What we need to do is look at this as a wake-up call to say, the storm isn't coming — the storm is here," Knight said. She noted that while a team of human experts might find 100 critical flaws in a year, Mythos can find thousands. "We certainly can't keep up now if they're using AI because it's so much devastatingly faster and more capable."

This sentiment is echoed by bank leadership. In his annual shareholder letter, JPMorgan Chase CEO Jamie Dimon warned that AI would likely "make threats worse and require significant defensive investments."

## Market Shockwaves and Economic Fallout

The revelation of Mythos’s capabilities sent immediate shockwaves through the financial markets. Investors, fearing that traditional cybersecurity tools might be rendered obsolete by autonomous AI hacking models, triggered a massive selloff. News related to the model contributed to a $2 trillion decline in enterprise software stocks.

Cybersecurity firms were hit particularly hard. Shares of Cloudflare (NET) dropped 8.62%, while CrowdStrike (CRWD) fell 7.46%. The market reaction reflects a growing concern that the very nature of software development and security is being upended. If an AI can find and fix vulnerabilities at a rate that dwarfs human capability, the value proposition of existing "legacy" security platforms is suddenly called into question.

## Strategic Marketing or Real Threat?

Despite the alarm bells ringing in Washington, not everyone is convinced that Mythos represents an existential threat. Some AI researchers suggest the model’s capabilities are being framed to benefit Anthropic’s brand as the "responsible" AI developer.

Malek Ben Sliman, a marketing lecturer at Columbia Business School, observed that curating the release "does allow them to look to be the protectors of this responsible AI, but it also is a great marketing and advertising tool."

Similarly, AI researcher and author Gary Marcus has called the Mythos hype "overblown." Writing on Substack, Marcus suggested that the industry may have been "played" by a highly effective proof-of-concept demo. "The demo was definitely proof of concept that we need to get our regulatory and technical house in order, but not the immediate threat the media and public was lead to believe," Marcus argued.

While the debate over the "true" novelty of Mythos continues, the reality for Wall Street is one of forced adaptation. With the Treasury and the Fed designating AI-driven attacks as a top systemic risk, the internal testing at major banks marks the beginning of a new era where the defense of the global financial system is increasingly handed over to the algorithms themselves.

While the government and Anthropic frame "Project Glasswing" as a vital defensive necessity, the timing and execution raise questions about whether this is a genuine security crisis or a highly orchestrated marketing event. By restricting access to a hand-picked group of "systemically important" institutions, Anthropic has created an aura of dangerous exclusivity that conveniently boosts its valuation while traditional cybersecurity firms take a $2 trillion hit. If the model is truly as volatile as regulators claim, placing it directly into the hands of the world’s most interconnected banks—rather than independent security researchers—seems less like a safeguard and more like an unregulated experiment in real-time systemic risk.
