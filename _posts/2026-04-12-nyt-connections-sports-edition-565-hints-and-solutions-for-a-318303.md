The daily ritual of word-based puzzles has become a staple for digital readers, and for sports enthusiasts, the stakes are specifically tailored to their domain. On April 11, 2026, the New York Times, in association with The Athletic, released "Connections: Sports Edition" puzzle No. 565. This version of the popular word game continues to challenge players to identify the common threads between 16 seemingly disparate terms, testing both their knowledge of the league rosters and their ability to spot linguistic traps.

## How the Game is Played

"Connections: Sports Edition" follows the familiar format established by the original New York Times "Connections" game. Players are presented with a grid of 16 words and must group them into four distinct categories. Each category contains four words that share a specific theme or relationship. According to data from Mashable, players are allowed only four mistakes before the game ends and the solutions are revealed.

To aid the process, the game interface on web browsers and mobile devices allows players to rearrange and shuffle the board, helping to break up visual patterns that might lead to incorrect associations. As players successfully identify categories, they are highlighted in specific colors that denote their difficulty level: yellow is the most straightforward, followed by green, blue, and finally purple, which is considered the most difficult or "tricky" category.

## Solving Puzzle No. 565: April 11, 2026

The puzzle released on April 11 featured a mix of team names, geographic indicators, and wordplay. Players who navigated the grid successfully found the following solutions:

The **Yellow Category**, titled "MLB Teams, Colloquially," consisted of the words **D-BACKS, JAYS, PHILS, and SOX**. This category was ranked as the easiest, relying on common abbreviations and nicknames for the Arizona Diamondbacks, Toronto Blue Jays, Philadelphia Phillies, and either the Boston Red Sox or Chicago White Sox.

The **Green Category** took players to the collegiate level with the theme "UCLA." The words were **ANGELES, CALIFORNIA, LOS, and UNIVERSITY**. While the individual words were simple, the challenge lay in recognizing that they formed the full name of the University of California, Los Angeles, rather than being part of different city-based categories.

The **Blue Category** focused on regional sports identity with "Can Follow Minnesota." The four words were **LYNX, UNITED, VIKINGS, and WILD**. This category required players to connect the professional teams associated with the state: the WNBA's Lynx, MLS's United, the NFL's Vikings, and the NHL's Wild.

Finally, the **Purple Category**, "Starts With Part of the Body," was the most complex solution of the day. The words were **ARMY, EARTHQUAKES, LEGACY, and LIVERPOOL**.

## Navigating the Tricky Categories

The difficulty of No. 565 largely stemmed from the purple and green categories. As noted by several gaming analysis sites, the "Starts With Part of the Body" category was particularly sneaky because the body parts were embedded within larger words. Players had to mentally strip away the suffixes to find **ARM** (Army), **EAR** (Earthquakes), **LEG** (Legacy), and **LIP** (Liverpool). This type of wordplay is a hallmark of the purple category, which often prioritizes linguistics over direct sports trivia.

The UCLA category also proved to be a hurdle for many. By breaking down the university's name into its individual components—Los, Angeles, California, and University—the puzzle creators forced players to separate these common words from potential associations with other cities or generic university terms.

Furthermore, the word "WILD" was identified as a potential "trap word" or red herring. Because "Wild" can be a descriptor in many contexts, players who didn't immediately connect it to the Minnesota Wild of the NHL risked wasting their limited mistakes trying to pair it with other words on the board.

## The Background of Sports Connections

"Connections: Sports Edition" was launched to provide a more targeted experience for fans of The Athletic and the New York Times' sports coverage. Much like Wordle and the original Connections, the game resets daily, creating a shared community experience where players often compare their scores and "grid" results on social media.

The association with The Athletic brings a level of specificity to the game that sets it apart from more general word puzzles. By focusing on team nicknames, specific regional franchises, and historical sports context, the game serves as a bridge between the Times' gaming wing and its deep bench of sports journalism.

While the immediate focus remains on the logistical nightmare facing players when they encounter a particularly tough purple category, the game remains a primary engagement tool for the platform, with hints and solutions being widely published daily to assist the gaming community in maintaining their winning streaks.

While "Connections: Sports Edition" is marketed as a test of athletic knowledge, the increasing reliance on linguistic trickery—like hiding "EAR" inside "EARTHQUAKES"—suggests the game is drifting away from its sporting roots and into the realm of generic wordplay. By prioritizing "gotcha" mechanics over genuine sports expertise, the New York Times and The Athletic risk alienating the very audience the game was designed for: the die-hard sports fan who values trivia over puns. This shift raises the question of whether the game is still a sports challenge or simply a branding exercise designed to drive daily engagement through frustration.
